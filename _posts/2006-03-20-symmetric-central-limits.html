--- layout: post title: 'Symmetric central limits' date: 2006-03-20 18:37:00 tags: [central limits, mathematics, statistics] --- Some progress on the problem I mentioned earlier, of density near the origin in central limits. I can now handle another fairly broad case, that of centrally symmetric distributions. In fact, for these distributions, a stronger statement about density near the origin can be made, applying to all bounded-radius balls rather than merely to sufficiently large ones:

Theorem: Let F be a centrally symmetric distribution with bounded support on Rd, and let Xi be independent draws from F. Then for any K > 0 and even n, P[|Σ0≤i<nXi| ≤ K] = Ω(n-d/2).

Proof: Consider Y = Σ0≤i<n/2Xi and Z = &Sigman/2≤i<n-Xi. Y and Z are independent, and (by symmetry) identically distributed. By an appropriate multidimensional central limit theorem (applied to the affine hull of the support of F), with constant probability both Y and Z are bounded by O(sqrt n). Partition the ball of radius O(sqrt n) into O(nd/2) subsets Sj, each of diameter at most K, and let pj be the probability that Y (or Z) belongs to Sj. If both Y and Z belong to the same Sj, then they are within distance K of each other, so P[|Σ0≤i<nXi| ≤ K] = P[|Y-Z| ≤ K] ≥ Σjpj2. But the sum of squares of a set of variables, that together add to a fixed value, is minimized when each variable is equal, so Σjpj2 = Ω(n-d/2).

As a corollary, the original statement of the problem, that there exists K such that the probability of being within distance K of the origin is Ω(n-d/2) for all n (not just all even n) holds for these distributions as well. For, if n is even, it follows from the theorem above, while if n is odd, by the triangle inequality |Σ0≤i<nXi| ≤ |Σ0≤i<n-1Xi|+|Xn-1| and we can choose K to be any constant greater than the maximum possible value of |Xi|. The example of a simple random walk on an integer lattice shows that this choice of K is the smallest possible.

ETA: The same argument seems to apply more generally to nonconstant radii, showing that P[|Σ0≤i<nXi| ≤ r] = Ω(rdn-d/2) whenever r = O(sqrt n) and either n is even or r > (1+ε)E[|Xi|] for some constant ε > 0.
