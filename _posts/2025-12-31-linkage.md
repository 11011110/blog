* [Quadratrix of Hippias, now a Good Article on Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratrix_of_Hippias) <span style="white-space:nowrap">([$$\mathbb{M}$$](https://mathstodon.xyz/@11011110/115787743170966140)).</span> This is a curve swept out by the crossing point of two moving lines, one rotating and one translating. The ancient Greeks discovered that if you have a copy of this curve already, you can use it to trisect angles and construct a square with the area of a given circle, two classical geometry problems that are impossible with compass and straightedge alone. [As I wrote on an earlier blog post]({{site.baseurl}}{% post_url 2025-03-22-ancient-greek-mathematics %}), you can also see curves like this when you take a photo of a spinning airplane propellor using a camera with a rolling shutter:

* [Doubly dual shuffles](https://dotat.at/@/2025-12-25-shuffle.html) <span style="white-space:nowrap">([$$\mathbb{M}$$](https://mathstodon.xyz/@svat/115794889200270140)),</span> four different ways of implementing the Fischer–Yates random permutation algorithm. The discussion goes into why you might choose one over another depending on whether you want to leave items in place after they're permuted once, or whether the stream of items to be permuted has unknown length.

* [Disproof of the odd Hadwiger conjecture](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.20392) <span style="white-space:nowrap">([$$\mathbb{M}$$](https://mathstodon.xyz/@DavidWood/115783214907901459)),</span> new preprint by Marcus Kühn, Lisa Sauermann, Raphael Steiner, and Yuval Wigderson. This is the one on the relation between clique minors (with paths of odd length between designated seed vertices) and coloring. They construct graphs for which the chromatic number is higher than the odd clique minor size by a factor of roughly $$\tfrac32$$.
